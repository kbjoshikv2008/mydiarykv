6.1 Numbers Tell Us Things

In this section, we explore how numbers can represent information about arrangements and patterns.

1

Arrangement Puzzles

Arrange stick figures or create a height arrangement so that the sequence of numbers (each representing the count of taller people in front) matches:

(a) 0, 1, 1, 2, 4, 1, 5

(b) 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0

(c) 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6

Solutions:

(a) This arrangement has the tallest person at the end, with progressively shorter people in front.

(b) All people are the same height in this arrangement.

(c) People are arranged in increasing order of height from front to back.

Tip: Try using actual objects or drawings to visualize these arrangements!

2

True or False Statements

For each statement, determine if it is Always True, Sometimes True, or Never True:

(a) If a person says '0', then they are the tallest in the group.

(b) If a person is the tallest, then their number is '0'.

(c) The first person's number is '0'.

Analysis:

(a) Sometimes True - A person saying '0' means no one in front is taller, but there could be people of equal height behind them.

(b) Always True - The tallest person has no one taller in front, so their number must be '0'.

(c) Sometimes True - The first person's number is '0' only if they are taller than everyone behind them.

6.2 Picking Parity

Explore the properties of even and odd numbers and how they interact in sums and differences.

3

Parity of Sums

Using your understanding of odd and even numbers, determine the parity of:

(a) Sum of 2 even numbers and 2 odd numbers

(b) Sum of 2 odd numbers and 3 even numbers

(c) Sum of 5 even numbers

(d) Sum of 8 odd numbers

Solutions:

(a) Even (even + even + odd + odd = even)

(b) Even (odd + odd + even + even + even = even)

(c) Even (any number of even numbers sum to even)

(d) Even (even number of odd numbers sum to even)

Remember: Even + Even = Even, Odd + Odd = Even, Even + Odd = Odd

4

Coin Problem

Lakpa has an odd number of ₹1 coins, an odd number of ₹5 coins, and an even number of ₹10 coins. He calculated the total as ₹205. Did he make a mistake? Explain why.

Analysis:

Yes, he made a mistake. Let's analyze:

Odd × ₹1 = odd amount

Odd × ₹5 = odd amount (since odd × odd = odd)

Even × ₹10 = even amount

Sum: odd + odd + even = even amount, but 205 is odd.

Therefore, there's a mistake in his calculation.

6.4 Virahänka-Fibonacci Numbers

Explore the fascinating sequence that appears throughout nature, art, and mathematics.

7

Sequence Exploration

The Virahänka-Fibonacci sequence begins: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, 55, ...

(a) What are the next 3 numbers in the sequence?

(b) What is the 100th odd number?

(c) How many rhythms are possible with 8 beats using short (1 beat) and long (2 beats) syllables?

Solutions:

(a) Next numbers: 89, 144, 233 (each term is the sum of the two previous terms)

(b) The 100th odd number is 199 (2×100 - 1)

(c) There are 34 rhythms with 8 beats (the 8th term in the Virahänka sequence)

6.5 Digits in Disguise

Become a math detective by solving cryptarithms where digits are replaced by letters.

8

Cryptarithm Solving

Solve the following cryptarithm:

   T
   T
+  T
---
  UT

Find the digits represented by T and U.

Solution:

T = 5, U = 1

Explanation: 5 + 5 + 5 = 15, so T=5 and UT=15, meaning U=1.

Mixed Practice

Test your understanding with these mixed problems from the chapter.

Problem 1

A light bulb is ON. Dorjee toggles its switch 77 times. Will the bulb be on or off? Why?

The bulb will be OFF. Starting from ON, after an odd number of toggles, the bulb is OFF. After an even number of toggles, the bulb is ON. 77 is odd, so the bulb will be OFF.

Problem 2

What is the parity of the sum of numbers from 1 to 100?

The sum is even. There are 50 odd numbers and 50 even numbers from 1 to 100. The sum of 50 odd numbers is even, and the sum of any number of even numbers is even, so the total is even.

Problem 3

Angaan wants to climb an 8-step staircase. He can take either 1 or 2 steps at a time. In how many different ways can he reach the top?

There are 34 ways. This is the 8th term in the Virahänka sequence (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, ...).
