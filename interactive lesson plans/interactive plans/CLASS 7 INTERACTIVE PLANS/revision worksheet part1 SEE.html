Chapter 5: Lines and Angles

Question 1
In Fig. 5.2, if ∠a = 120°, find ∠b, ∠c, and ∠d without measuring.

Solution:

∠a and ∠b are linear pairs, so ∠a + ∠b = 180°.

Given ∠a = 120°, then ∠b = 180° - 120° = 60°.

∠b and ∠c are linear pairs, so ∠c = 180° - 60° = 120°.

∠c and ∠d are linear pairs, so ∠d = 180° - 120° = 60°.

Alternatively, ∠a and ∠c are vertically opposite angles and equal (120°).

∠b and ∠d are vertically opposite angles and equal (60°).

Question 2
Can two straight lines intersect at more than one point? Explain.

Solution:

No, two distinct straight lines can intersect at most at one point.

If they intersect at two points, they would coincide and be the same line.

Question 3
What are perpendicular lines? Give an example.

Solution:

Perpendicular lines are lines that intersect at right angles (90°).

Example: The adjacent edges of a square sheet of paper are perpendicular.

Question 4
Define parallel lines. Name some parallel lines in your classroom.

Solution:

Parallel lines are lines in the same plane that never meet, no matter how far extended.

Example: Opposite edges of a ruler, railway tracks (in a plane), etc.

Question 5
In Fig. 5.14, line t is a transversal intersecting lines l and m. If ∠1 = 70°, find ∠5 (corresponding angle).

Solution:

When a transversal intersects two parallel lines, corresponding angles are equal.

Thus, ∠1 and ∠5 are corresponding angles, so ∠5 = ∠1 = 70°.

Question 6
How can you draw a pair of parallel lines using a ruler and set square?

Solution:

  1. Draw a line l using a ruler.
  2. Place the set square along line l.
  3. Slide the set square along the ruler and draw another line m along the set square's edge.
  4. Lines l and m are parallel because they are both perpendicular to the ruler's edge.
Question 7
In Fig. 5.25, if ∠f = 120°, find its alternate angle ∠d.

Solution:

Alternate angles are equal when lines are parallel.

∠f and ∠b are corresponding angles, so ∠b = 120°.

∠b and ∠d are vertically opposite angles, so ∠d = 120°.

Thus, ∠d = 120°.

Question 8
In Fig. 5.26, if ∠6 = 135°, find ∠2, ∠4, and ∠8.

Solution:

∠6 and ∠2 are corresponding angles, so ∠2 = 135°.

∠6 and ∠8 are vertically opposite angles, so ∠8 = 135°.

∠8 and ∠4 are corresponding angles, so ∠4 = 135°.

Question 9
In Fig. 5.27, if ∠a = 120° and ∠f = 70°, are lines l and m parallel?

Solution:

∠a and ∠b are linear pairs, so ∠b = 60°.

∠b and ∠f are corresponding angles. If lines were parallel, they should be equal.

But ∠b = 60° and ∠f = 70°, so they are not equal.

Thus, lines l and m are not parallel.

Question 10
In Fig. 5.30, find the marked angles.

Solution:

(Note: Since Fig. 5.30 is not fully described, we assume a typical problem with parallel lines and a transversal.)

If one angle is given, use corresponding, alternate, and linear pair properties to find others.

Example: If one angle is 50°, then its corresponding angle is 50°, its alternate angle is 50°, and its linear pair is 130°.

Chapter 6: Number Play

Question 11
In the height arrangement, if the sequence is 0,1,1,2,4,1,5, what does it mean?

Solution:

Each child calls out the number of children in front who are taller.

Sequence 0,1,1,2,4,1,5 means:

  • First child: no one taller in front.
  • Second child: 1 taller in front.
  • Third child: 1 taller in front.
  • Fourth child: 2 taller in front.
  • Fifth child: 4 taller in front.
  • Sixth child: 1 taller in front.
  • Seventh child: 5 taller in front.
Question 12
Kishor has 5 boxes and odd-numbered cards. Can he fill them to sum to 30?

Solution:

No, because the sum of 5 odd numbers is always odd.

30 is even, so it is impossible.

Question 13
Martin and Maria are one year apart. Can their ages sum to 112?

Solution:

No, because consecutive numbers have opposite parity.

Sum of an even and an odd number is odd.

112 is even, so it is impossible.

Question 14
What is the 100th odd number?

Solution:

The nth odd number is given by 2n - 1.

So, 100th odd number = 2 × 100 - 1 = 199.

Question 15
In a 3×3 grid using numbers 1-9, what is the magic sum?

Solution:

The sum of numbers 1 to 9 is 45.

In a magic square, each row sum is equal, and there are 3 rows.

So, magic sum = 45 ÷ 3 = 15.

Question 16
What is the centre number in a 3×3 magic square using 1-9?

Solution:

The centre number must be 5.

Reason: The magic sum is 15, and the centre number is involved in four sums (row, column, two diagonals).

After trying possibilities, only 5 works.

Question 17
How many rhythms of 8 beats are possible using short (1 beat) and long (2 beats) syllables?

Solution:

This is the Virahanka sequence: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, ...

The 8th term is 34.

So, there are 34 rhythms.

Question 18
Solve the cryptarithm: U + U + U = TU (each letter a digit).

Solution:

U + U + U = 3U = TU, where TU is a two-digit number.

The units digit of 3U is U, so U must be 5 (since 3×5=15).

Then 3×5=15, so T=1, U=5.

Thus, 5+5+5=15.

Question 19
A light bulb is ON. Toggled 77 times. Is it ON or OFF?

Solution:

Starting from ON, after an odd number of toggles, the bulb is OFF.

77 is odd, so the bulb is OFF.

Question 20
50 loose sheets, each printed on both sides. Can the sum of page numbers be 6000?

Solution:

Each sheet has two page numbers: one odd, one even.

Sum of an odd and an even number is odd.

Sum of 50 such pairs is 50 × (odd) = even (since 50 is even).

6000 is even, so it is possible.

(However, the actual sum depends on the starting page. The condition is parity-possible.)

Chapter 7: Triangles

Question 21
Construct an equilateral triangle of side 4 cm.

Solution:

  1. Draw a line segment AB = 4 cm.
  2. With A as centre, radius 4 cm, draw an arc.
  3. With B as centre, radius 4 cm, draw another arc to intersect the first arc at C.
  4. Join AC and BC.
  5. ΔABC is the required equilateral triangle.
Question 22
Construct a triangle with sides 4 cm, 5 cm, 6 cm.

Solution:

  1. Draw base AB = 4 cm.
  2. With A as centre, radius 5 cm, draw an arc.
  3. With B as centre, radius 6 cm, draw another arc to intersect the first arc at C.
  4. Join AC and BC.
  5. ΔABC is the required triangle.
Question 23
Check if a triangle with sides 10 cm, 15 cm, 30 cm exists.

Solution:

Use triangle inequality: each side must be less than the sum of the other two.

Check: 30 < 10+15? 30 < 25? No.

So, such a triangle does not exist.

Question 24
Construct ΔABC with AB=5 cm, AC=4 cm, and ∠A=45°.

Solution:

  1. Draw AB=5 cm.
  2. At A, construct an angle of 45° using protractor or compass.
  3. On the arm of the angle, mark C such that AC=4 cm.
  4. Join BC.
  5. ΔABC is the required triangle.
Question 25
Construct ΔABC with AB=5 cm, ∠A=45°, ∠B=80°.

Solution:

  1. Draw AB=5 cm.
  2. At A, construct an angle of 45°.
  3. At B, construct an angle of 80°.
  4. Let the arms of these angles intersect at C.
  5. ΔABC is the required triangle.
Question 26
In a triangle, two angles are 50° and 70°. Find the third angle.

Solution:

Sum of angles in a triangle = 180°.

Third angle = 180° - (50° + 70°) = 60°.

Question 27
In ΔABC, ∠A=50°, ∠B=60°. Find the exterior angle at C.

Solution:

∠C = 180° - (50°+60°) = 70°.

Exterior angle at C = 180° - ∠C = 180° - 70° = 110°.

Alternatively, exterior angle = sum of opposite interior angles = 50°+60°=110°.

Question 28
Construct an altitude from vertex A to side BC in a triangle.

Solution:

  1. Draw triangle ABC.
  2. Place a ruler along BC.
  3. Use a set square perpendicular to the ruler, slide it until the perpendicular edge passes through A.
  4. Draw the line from A perpendicular to BC, meeting BC at D.
  5. AD is the altitude.
Question 29
Classify triangles based on angles.

Solution:

  • Acute-angled triangle: all angles < 90°.
  • Right-angled triangle: one angle = 90°.
  • Obtuse-angled triangle: one angle > 90°.
Question 30
Construct a right-angled triangle with hypotenuse AC=5 cm.

Solution:

  1. Draw a line segment AC=5 cm.
  2. Find the midpoint O of AC (since in a right triangle, the midpoint of the hypotenuse is the circumcentre).
  3. With O as centre, radius OA=2.5 cm, draw a semicircle on AC.
  4. Choose any point B on the semicircle (other than A and C).
  5. Join AB and BC.
  6. ΔABC is a right-angled triangle with ∠B=90°.

Chapter 8: Fractions

Question 31
Aaron walks 3 km in 1 hour. How far in 1/5 hour?

Solution:

Distance = speed × time = 3 × 1/5 = 3/5 km.

Question 32
Multiply 2/5 and 3/4.

Solution:

2/5 × 3/4 = (2×3)/(5×4) = 6/20 = 3/10.

Question 33
Divide 2/3 by 3/5.

Solution:

2/3 ÷ 3/5 = 2/3 × 5/3 = (2×5)/(3×3) = 10/9.

Question 34
Leena used 1/4 litre milk for 5 cups of tea. How much milk per cup?

Solution:

Milk per cup = total milk ÷ number of cups = (1/4) ÷ 5 = 1/4 × 1/5 = 1/20 litre.

Question 35
Cover 7½ sq. units with square bricks of side 1/5 unit. How many bricks?

Solution:

Area of one brick = (1/5)×(1/5) = 1/25 sq. units.

Number of bricks = total area ÷ area per brick = (15/2) ÷ (1/25) = 15/2 × 25/1 = 375/2 = 187.5 bricks.

But since bricks are whole, we need 188 bricks.

Question 36
Four fountains fill a cistern in 1 day, 1/2 day, 1/4 day, and 1/5 day. Together, how long?

Solution:

In one day, each fills: 1, 2, 4, and 5 times respectively.

Together in one day: 1+2+4+5 = 12 times.

So, time to fill once = 1/12 day.

Question 37
In Fig. 8.4, shaded region is what fraction of the whole square?

Solution:

(Assuming the figure shows a square divided into smaller parts.)

If the shaded region is 3 out of 32 equal parts, then fraction = 3/32.

Question 38
Multiply 7 × 3/5 and convert to mixed fraction.

Solution:

7 × 3/5 = 21/5 = 4⅕.

Question 39
Divide 1/5 ÷ 1/2.

Solution:

1/5 ÷ 1/2 = 1/5 × 2/1 = 2/5.

Question 40
Mira read 1/5 and 3/10 of a 400-page novel. How many pages left?

Solution:

Pages read = 400 × (1/5 + 3/10) = 400 × (2/10 + 3/10) = 400 × 5/10 = 400 × 1/2 = 200 pages.

Pages left = 400 - 200 = 200 pages.
