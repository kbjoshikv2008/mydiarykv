Activity 8: 3D Geometry Laboratory

Measuring Shortest Distance Between Skew Lines

Mathematics Laboratory Manual

Objective

To measure the shortest distance between two skew lines in three-dimensional space and verify it analytically using vector methods.

Materials Required

  • Plywood board (30 cm × 20 cm)
  • Squared paper/graph paper
  • Three wooden blocks (2 cm × 2 cm × 2 cm each)
  • One wooden block (2 cm × 2 cm × 4 cm)
  • Wires of different lengths
  • Set squares
  • Adhesive (glue)
  • Pen/pencil
  • Ruler/measuring scale

Method of Construction

  1. Paste a squared paper on the plywood board to create a coordinate plane.
  2. Draw two perpendicular lines OA and OB to represent the x-axis and y-axis respectively.
  3. Label the three 2 cm cubes as Blocks I, II, and III. Label the 2 cm × 2 cm × 4 cm block as Block IV.
  4. Position the blocks as follows:
    • Block I: Base center at (2, 2) with height 2 cm
    • Block II: Base center at (1, 6) with height 2 cm
    • Block III: Base center at (7, 6) with height 2 cm
    • Block IV: Base center at (6, 2) with height 4 cm
  5. Connect the centers of Block I and Block III with a wire (Line PQ).
  6. Connect the top centers of Block II and Block IV with another wire (Line RS).
  7. These two wires represent skew lines in 3D space (non-parallel, non-intersecting).
  8. Use another wire to connect the two skew lines perpendicularly and measure this distance.

Demonstration

  1. Place a set-square such that one perpendicular side aligns with wire PQ (first skew line).
  2. Slide the set-square along PQ until the other perpendicular side just touches the second wire RS.
  3. The distance between the two lines at this point represents the shortest distance between the skew lines.
  4. Measure this distance using the set-square and scale.
  5. Record this measurement for comparison with analytical results.

Analytical Calculation

To find the shortest distance between two skew lines, we use the vector formula:

Shortest Distance = |(a₂ - a₁) · (b₁ × b₂)| / |b₁ × b₂|

Step 1: Find direction vectors

For line PQ: Direction vector b₁ = Q - P = (7-2, 6-2, 0-0) = (5, 4, 0)

For line RS: Direction vector b₂ = S - R = (6-1, 2-6, 4-2) = (5, -4, 2)

Step 2: Find vector between points on lines

a₂ - a₁ = R - P = (1-2, 6-2, 2-0) = (-1, 4, 2)

Step 3: Calculate cross product b₁ × b₂

b₁ × b₂ = |i j k|

|5 4 0| = i(4×2 - 0×(-4)) - j(5×2 - 0×5) + k(5×(-4) - 4×5)

|5 -4 2|

= i(8 - 0) - j(10 - 0) + k(-20 - 20)

= (8, -10, -40)

Step 4: Calculate |b₁ × b₂|

|b₁ × b₂| = √(8² + (-10)² + (-40)²) = √(64 + 100 + 1600) = √1764 = 42

Step 5: Calculate dot product (a₂ - a₁) · (b₁ × b₂)

(-1, 4, 2) · (8, -10, -40) = (-1×8) + (4×-10) + (2×-40) = -8 - 40 - 80 = -128

Step 6: Apply formula

Shortest distance = | -128 | / 42 = 128 / 42 = 64 / 21 ≈ 3.05 cm

The results obtained by both methods are approximately equal, verifying the concept of shortest distance between skew lines.

Application

  • Visualizing the shortest distance between two lines in space
  • Connecting theoretical vector mathematics with practical measurement
  • Developing spatial reasoning and 3D visualization skills
  • Applying cross product and dot product concepts in a practical scenario
  • Understanding real-world applications in architecture, engineering, and computer graphics
