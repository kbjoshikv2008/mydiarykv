1. Introduction to Triangles

A triangle is a closed figure formed by three line segments joining three non-collinear points.
Parts of a Triangle:
  • Vertices: The three corner points (A, B, C)
  • Sides: The three line segments (AB, BC, CA)
  • Angles: The three angles formed at the vertices (∠A, ∠B, ∠C)
Example:
In triangle ABC, the vertices are A, B, and C. The sides are AB, BC, and CA. The angles are ∠A, ∠B, and ∠C.

2. Constructing Triangles

2.1 Equilateral Triangles

An equilateral triangle has all three sides equal in length and all three angles equal to 60°.
1 Draw the base AB of the given length (say 4 cm).
2 With A as center and radius 4 cm, draw an arc.
3 With B as center and radius 4 cm, draw another arc to intersect the first arc at C.
4 Join AC and BC to complete triangle ABC.

2.2 Triangles with Three Given Sides

1 Draw the base with one of the given lengths.
2 From one endpoint, draw an arc with radius equal to the second side.
3 From the other endpoint, draw an arc with radius equal to the third side.
4 The intersection point of the two arcs is the third vertex.
5 Join this point to both endpoints of the base.
Example:
Construct a triangle with sides 4 cm, 5 cm, and 6 cm.
  1. Draw base AB = 4 cm
  2. With A as center, radius 5 cm, draw an arc
  3. With B as center, radius 6 cm, draw another arc to intersect the first arc at C
  4. Join AC and BC

3. Triangle Inequality

Triangle Inequality Theorem: For any three lengths to form a triangle, each length must be less than the sum of the other two lengths.
To check if three lengths a, b, c can form a triangle, verify:
  • a < b + c
  • b < a + c
  • c < a + b
If all three conditions are satisfied, a triangle is possible.
Example 1:
Check if a triangle with sides 3 cm, 4 cm, and 8 cm is possible.
3 < 4 + 8 = 12 ✓
4 < 3 + 8 = 11 ✓
8 < 3 + 4 = 7 ✗
Since 8 > 3 + 4, a triangle is not possible.
Example 2:
Check if a triangle with sides 5 cm, 6 cm, and 7 cm is possible.
5 < 6 + 7 = 13 ✓
6 < 5 + 7 = 12 ✓
7 < 5 + 6 = 11 ✓
Since all conditions are satisfied, a triangle is possible.

4. Angle Sum Property

Angle Sum Property: The sum of the three angles of any triangle is always 180°.
In triangle ABC, ∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
Example 1:
In a triangle, if two angles are 45° and 60°, find the third angle.
Third angle = 180° - (45° + 60°) = 180° - 105° = 75°
Example 2:
In an equilateral triangle, each angle = 180° ÷ 3 = 60°

Important Points:

  • If two angles of a triangle are known, the third can be found using the angle sum property.
  • In a right triangle, the two acute angles are complementary (sum to 90°).
  • In an isosceles triangle, the base angles are equal.

5. Exterior Angles

An exterior angle of a triangle is formed when one side is extended. It is adjacent to an interior angle.
Exterior Angle Theorem: An exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the two interior opposite angles.
Example:
In triangle ABC, if ∠A = 50° and ∠B = 60°, find the exterior angle at C.
Exterior angle at C = ∠A + ∠B = 50° + 60° = 110°
Properties of Exterior Angles:
  • An exterior angle is equal to the sum of the two interior opposite angles.
  • An exterior angle and its adjacent interior angle form a linear pair (sum to 180°).
  • A triangle has 6 exterior angles (2 at each vertex).

6. Altitudes of Triangles

An altitude of a triangle is a perpendicular line segment from a vertex to the line containing the opposite side.
Properties of Altitudes:
  • Every triangle has three altitudes.
  • Altitudes may lie inside or outside the triangle.
  • In an acute triangle, all altitudes lie inside the triangle.
  • In a right triangle, two altitudes are the legs of the triangle.
  • In an obtuse triangle, two altitudes lie outside the triangle.
1 Place the ruler along the base.
2 Place the set square on the ruler with its perpendicular edge touching the vertex.
3 Slide the set square until its perpendicular edge passes through the vertex.
4 Draw the line along the perpendicular edge from the vertex to the base.

7. Classification of Triangles

Classification Based On Types Properties
By Sides Length of sides Equilateral All sides equal, all angles 60°
Isosceles Two sides equal, two angles equal
Scalene All sides different, all angles different
By Angles Measure of angles Acute All angles less than 90°
Right One angle exactly 90°
Obtuse One angle greater than 90°

Exercises

Exercise 1: Triangle Construction

  1. An equilateral triangle with side 5 cm
  2. A triangle with sides 4 cm, 5 cm, and 6 cm

  1. 2 cm, 3 cm, 5 cm
  2. 3 cm, 4 cm, 5 cm

1. Find the third angle of a triangle when two angles are:
  1. 45° and 75°
  2. 90° and 30°
  3. 110° and 40°

1. In a triangle, two angles are 55° and 65°. Find the measure of the exterior angle at the third vertex.

Exercise 4: Classification

Classify the following triangles based on sides and angles:
  1. Triangle with sides 5 cm, 5 cm, 5 cm
  2. Triangle with angles 90°, 45°, 45°
  3. Triangle with sides 6 cm, 8 cm, 10 cm

Exercise 5: Word Problems

1. The angles of a triangle are in the ratio 2:3:4. Find the measure of each angle.
2. In an isosceles triangle, the base is 5 cm and each of the equal sides is 6 cm. Find its perimeter.
3. One angle of a triangle is 60° and the other two angles are equal. Find the measure of each of the equal angles.

Important Formulas & Theorems

Formulas:

  • Angle sum of triangle: ∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
  • Exterior angle: ∠exterior = ∠A + ∠B (where ∠A and ∠B are interior opposite angles)
  • Perimeter of triangle = a + b + c

Theorems:

  • Triangle Inequality: a + b > c, b + c > a, c + a > b
  • Angle Sum Property: Sum of angles = 180°
  • Exterior Angle Theorem: Exterior angle = Sum of interior opposite angles

Construction Tips:

  • Always use a sharp pencil for accurate constructions
  • Keep the compass tight to prevent slipping
  • Draw light construction lines that can be erased later
  • Label all points clearly
